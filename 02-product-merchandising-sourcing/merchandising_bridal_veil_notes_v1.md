# Bridal Veil Merchandising Notes v1

Source lens: James Clark, *Fashion Merchandising: Principles and Practice*, 2nd ed. This is a translated working note for a small bridal veil DTC brand, not a general textbook summary.

## 1. Merchandising Is the Bridge Between Product and Money

In plain language: merchandising is the discipline that prevents "pretty products" from becoming dead inventory.

For this brand, merchandising should connect:

- What brides want
- What the brand wants to stand for
- What each SKU costs
- How much cash is available
- How fast inventory can be replenished
- Which products deserve more stock
- Which products should be paused, improved, or exited

The merchandiser's job is not just to pick products. The job is to make the product range commercially coherent.

## 2. Research and Analysis

Book concept: good merchandising starts with research and analysis before buying decisions.

Bridal veil translation:

Before buying more inventory, review:

- Product page sessions
- Conversion rate
- Units sold
- Sell-through
- Gross margin
- Returns or complaints
- Content performance
- Search intent
- Inventory left
- Lead time

The key is not only "what sold?" but "what should have sold?"

Example:

- A veil sold out in two weeks. That is good, but it may also mean the initial buy was too low.
- A veil had many page views but low conversion. That may be a product page, price, photo, trust, or color clarity problem.
- A veil had low traffic but high conversion. That may be a hidden winner that needs more content and internal links.

## 3. Product Range Strategy

Book concept: a product range should be balanced, not just a list of products.

Bridal veil translation:

The launch range needs roles:

- Traffic driver: Simple Cathedral Veil
- Upgrade volume: Pearl Scatter Cathedral Veil
- SEO category door: Affordable Lace Mantilla Veil
- Price anchor: Premium Lace Cathedral Veil
- City hall / short option: Satin Edge Mid-Length Veil
- Content hook: Pearl Puff Short Veil
- Editorial visual: 3D Floral / Ruffled visual samples

The range is healthy if it covers:

- Entry price
- Mid price
- Premium price
- Simple
- Pearl
- Lace
- Long
- Short
- Ceremony
- Second look
- SEO demand
- Brand memory

## 4. Budgeting

Book concept: merchandising works from sales, stock, margin, and markdown budgets.

Bridal veil translation:

Your buying budget should not be:

> I like this SKU, so I will buy more.

It should be:

> How much cash can I safely turn into inventory without starving marketing, content, software, and runway?

For this brand, the practical budget layers are:

- Starting cash
- Initial inventory cash
- Sample and content cash
- Monthly operating cash
- Marketing cash
- Emergency buffer
- Reorder cash

Buying inventory is not only a margin decision. It is a cash-flow decision.

## 5. Open-to-Buy

Book concept: open-to-buy is the amount still available to spend on product within a planned stock/sales budget.

Plain language:

Open-to-buy is the money you are still allowed to spend on inventory.

Bridal veil formula:

```text
Open-to-buy cash =
available cash
- required operating runway
- marketing/content reserve
- emergency buffer
- already committed inventory orders
```

Example:

```text
Cash available: $8,000
Required operating reserve: $2,000
Marketing/content reserve: $1,500
Emergency buffer: $1,000
Already committed inventory: $0

Open-to-buy = $3,500
```

This means even if the spreadsheet says you "should" reorder $5,000 of stock, you cannot do it unless cash allows it.

## 6. Range Planning

Book concept: range planning decides how the total product offer is built across categories, price points, options, and depth.

Bridal veil translation:

Range planning asks:

- How many SKUs should exist?
- Which roles are missing?
- Are we overexposed to one aesthetic?
- Are too many SKUs competing for the same bride?
- Does each product have a clear reason to exist?
- Do we have too much stock in low-confidence products?

For the launch range, avoid adding SKUs unless they fill a true gap.

Bad reason to add a SKU:

- It is cute.
- Competitors have it.
- Maybe someone will want it.

Good reason to add a SKU:

- It captures a proven search term.
- It fills a missing price band.
- It serves a distinct bride occasion.
- It supports a high-performing content angle.
- It improves a hero SKU bundle or upsell path.

## 7. Initial Buy

Initial buy is the first inventory commitment.

For a new bridal veil brand, the initial buy is not meant to maximize sales. It is meant to buy information.

The first buy should answer:

- Which SKU gets clicks?
- Which SKU converts?
- Which SKU creates content engagement?
- Which price feels acceptable?
- Which product page needs work?
- Which SKU deserves reorder capital?

Current lean recommendation:

```text
50-60 sellable units
+ 8-10 sample / content / creator buffer
= about 60-70 total units
```

This aligns with the current Base model:

```text
M1-M3 Base units: 33
M1-M4 Base units: 59
```

So 50-60 sellable units can work for a lean launch if replenishment is monitored from month 1.

## 8. Sell-Through

Sell-through shows how much of the bought stock has sold.

```text
Sell-through = units sold / initial stock
```

Example:

```text
Simple Cathedral:
Initial stock: 16
Sold: 8
Sell-through: 50%
```

Interpretation:

- High sell-through + good margin = likely reorder
- High sell-through + low traffic = hidden winner
- Low sell-through + high traffic = conversion problem
- Low sell-through + low traffic = weak SKU or weak exposure

For launch, sell-through thresholds can be:

- 25% sold: early read
- 40-50% sold: watch closely
- 60-70% sold: prepare reorder if other metrics support it
- 80%+ sold: urgent decision

## 9. Weeks of Supply

Weeks of supply tells how long stock will last at the current sales speed.

```text
Weeks of supply = current sellable inventory / average weekly units sold
```

Example:

```text
Inventory left: 12
Average weekly sales: 3
Weeks of supply: 4
```

If replenishment takes four weeks including production and logistics, this SKU is already at risk.

## 10. Reorder Point

Reorder point is the inventory level that triggers a reorder.

For this brand:

```text
Reorder point =
expected sales during lead time
+ safety stock
```

If lead time is one month:

```text
Monthly demand: 12
Safety stock: 4
Reorder point: 16
```

This is why a core SKU can be "too low" even if it has not sold out.

## 11. Reorder Quantity

Reorder quantity is how many units to buy next.

It should depend on:

- Role: core vs test vs editorial
- Sales speed
- Sell-through
- Margin
- Return/complaint risk
- Lead time
- Cash available
- Upcoming content or campaign plans

Example:

Simple Cathedral:

- Core SKU
- High intent
- Low cost
- Broad appeal

Reorder can be 20-30 units once validated.

Premium Lace Cathedral:

- Higher cost
- Narrower appeal
- Price anchor

Reorder may be 5-8 units or zero until proven.

## 12. Allocation

Book concept: allocation is where stock goes across stores/channels.

Bridal veil DTC translation:

You do not have physical stores yet, but you still allocate inventory across uses:

- Sellable stock
- Shoot samples
- Creator seeding
- Stylist/fit sample
- Quality replacement buffer
- Editorial-only sample

If you do not separate these, you will think you have more sellable inventory than you really do.

## 13. E-Commerce Merchandising

Book concept: e-commerce adds traffic, conversion, search behavior, reviews, site navigation, and digital merchandising to the traditional stock/sales view.

Bridal veil translation:

A product is not only a unit of inventory. It is also:

- A search landing page
- A Pinterest image
- A TikTok content object
- A dress-pairing answer
- A trust signal
- A review generator
- A styling tool

Weekly review must include both product and site metrics:

- Product sessions
- Add to cart rate
- Conversion rate
- Search source
- Internal links
- Image performance
- Review count
- Return reasons

## 14. SKU Lifecycle

Each SKU should have a status:

- Concept
- Sample
- Launch test
- Active
- Core
- Reorder watch
- Pause
- Exit
- Editorial only

Example:

Simple Cathedral starts as Launch Test. If it sells through and converts well, it becomes Core.

3D Floral starts as Editorial Only. It should not be judged by sell-through unless it becomes a sellable product.

## 15. The System We Eventually Need

The final merchandising system should produce these outputs:

- Current inventory by SKU
- True sellable inventory
- Weeks of supply
- Sell-through
- Product conversion status
- Reorder point
- Reorder recommendation
- Suggested reorder quantity
- Cash required
- Stockout risk
- SKU action: reorder / watch / improve page / push content / pause / exit

The system should not blindly optimize for units sold. It must understand SKU role.

## 16. Core Rule for This Brand

Use small inventory to learn, but do not let core winners go out of stock.

The operating logic:

```text
Test with low stock.
Promote with content.
Read sell-through and conversion.
Reorder only validated demand.
Protect cash.
Protect hero SKUs.
Exit weak stock without emotion.
```
